There seems to be no correlation between card craft cost and rating which suggests that outlandishly expensive decks to craft are not necessarily the most popular or successful of decks. One of the factors as to why this is the case is that not everyone can make the expensive decks so they are rated poorly. However the most popular decks, at least from this scatter plot, seem to lie between the cost of 0 to 20000 dust.

Dust in this case is the currency used to craft cards. Common cards craft for 40 dust, rare for 100 dust, epic for 400 dust, and legendary for 1600 dust.

A deck is constructed of 30 of these cards. The reason why we see outrageously expensive crafting costs are perhaps theory crafted decks or joke decks posted on Hearthpwn.

Looking at the average cost distribution of deck costs for classes it matches the class identity that blizzard ascribed to the classes. Generally warrior decks are costly because the class identity for warrior is control. Control is a playstyle in which the cards in your deck are made to "control" the board. This usually includes but is not limited to board wipes and minion removal spells. Since board wipes and minion removal spells are efficient, they remove minions that cost more mana than the removal spell to summon. Warriors often run expensive finisher cards to win the game once they clear the board. Hunter on the other hand plays an aggressive style. Normally the mana costs of the cards that hunter decks run are cheap because the game plan for a hunter is to set up a board and rush the opponent down before the opponent can set up a defense. Hunters run a more minion heavy deck with a few finisher spells while warriors run a spell heavy deck with big minion finishers. Quite the opposite playstyle.

def iterative_fit(algorithm, X_train, y_train, X_test, y_test, max_epochs):
    model = algorithm
    scores = {'train':[], 'test':[]}
    for i in range(max_epochs):# fit 1 epoch (=1 iteration through whole dataset)
        model.partial_fit(X_train, y_train)
        train_score = model.score(X_train, y_train)
        test_score = model.score(X_test, y_test)
        scores['train'].append(train_score)
        scores['test'].append(test_score)
    return scores, model

Here I try to run a regression on the data to predict the mana cost of a card given the health and attack stats of the card. According to the R score only 40% of the variability can be explained with the health and attack stats. This makes sense as there are a few other non-numerical factors that go into play when deciding the mana cost of a card. For example, Wilfred Fizzlebang, a warlock class legendary card, is a 6 mana cost 4 attack 4 health minion. Given the stat line one would assume that Wilfred would cost 4 mana; however the cost of the card is 6 mana due to its effect of making the next card one draws from the warlock hero power cost 0 mana. The effect of the card is potent so in order to balance the Wilfred they made the minion cost 2 mana more than any usual minion with a 4/4 stat line.
